Method, sources & confidence

Visibility engine

Detection is modelled with the contrast‑threshold method of Crumey (2014, MNRAS 442, 2600), built on Blackwell's (1946) laboratory data — not raw catalogue magnitude, and not average surface brightness alone. Naked‑eye stellar limit uses m0 = 0.383·µsky − 1.44 − 2.5·log F; extended objects use the Ricco‑area threshold (Eq. 62); binoculars and telescopes use the telescopic point‑source limit (Eq. 69, validated by the paper against Bowen's Mount Wilson data) with the zero‑background cut‑off, sweeping magnification for the optimum. The same measured sky brightness (µsky) that colours the map drives every verdict. These were checked against the paper's own worked values (naked‑eye limit 6.17 vs 6.18; M33 correctly a hard naked‑eye object) and against known aperture limits (200 mm → mag 14.2).

The honest caveats

The model assumes a roughly uniform object. Real deep‑sky objects aren't: a bright core beats its average surface brightness. A per‑type concentration factor partly corrects this (globulars, planetaries and ellipticals are treated as more concentrated than their catalogue size), but galaxy verdicts carry lower confidence and are flagged. Extinction near the horizon is applied as Δm = k·(airmass−1). Verdicts are calibrated planning estimates, not guarantees — dark adaptation, transparency and experience all move the line.
